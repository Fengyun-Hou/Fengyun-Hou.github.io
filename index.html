About Me

I am a PhD researcher in the School of Psychological Science at the University of Bristol, supervised by Professor Nina Kazanina, Professor Markus Damian, and Dr Alexander Anderson. My doctoral research centres on relational representations — exploring how the mind encodes and organises structured relations across visual perception, language processing, and memory.

Prior to joining Bristol, I completed an MA in Psycholinguistics at Beijing Language and Culture University, where I investigated interference effects of orthographic cues on second-language Chinese character learning. I also served as a Research Assistant at the Institute of Psychology, Chinese Academy of Sciences, working on Mandarin spoken word production and recognition.

My work combines EEG (frequency-tagging), behavioural experiments, and computational approaches to understand how humans extract relational meaning from language and the world. I am particularly interested in how minimal relational information shapes memory organisation and the neural signatures of compound word processing in Mandarin Chinese.

Beyond research, I teach Research Methods in Psychology and have taught Chinese Calligraphy and Mandarin as a Second Language. I speak Mandarin, Cantonese, and English, and program in Python, R, and MATLAB.
