Neural Mechanisms of Bilingual Language Processing

Active
2023 - Present Funded by: [Funding Body] ‚Ä¢ ¬£[Amount]

This project investigates how bilingual speakers process their two languages at the neural level using multimodal neuroimaging approaches. We explore language switching, cross-linguistic influence, and the neural networks involved in multilingual comprehension, with particular focus on executive control mechanisms and language-specific vs. shared neural resources.

Research Questions:

  • How do bilingual brains manage multiple language systems during comprehension?
  • What neural mechanisms underlie language switching costs?
  • How does age of acquisition affect neural language representation?

Methods:

fMRI EEG Behavioral Experiments Eye-tracking

Participants:

Semantic Processing in Context: Predictive Mechanisms

Active
2022 - Present Collaborative project with University of Cambridge

Examining how contextual information influences semantic processing and word recognition, with particular focus on predictive coding mechanisms in language comprehension. This project uses high-temporal-resolution EEG to track the dynamics of semantic prediction and integration.

Key Findings:

  • N400 component shows graded responses to semantic predictability
  • Individual differences in working memory affect prediction strength
  • Cross-linguistic differences in predictive processing patterns
ERP Eye-tracking Computational Modeling

2024 - Present Collaborative project with Bristol Language Development Lab

Longitudinal investigation of how multilingual language processing develops from childhood through adulthood, focusing on critical periods, neural plasticity, and the emergence of language-specific vs. universal processing mechanisms.

Completed
2020 - 2022 Postdoctoral Research Project

Longitudinal study investigating language development patterns in children exposed to multiple languages from early age, focusing on cognitive advantages, neural plasticity, and the relationship between multilingual experience and executive function development.

Key Contributions:

Cross-linguistic Syntactic Processing: Universal vs. Language-specific Mechanisms

Completed
2019 - 2022 PhD Dissertation Project

Investigated universal and language-specific aspects of syntactic processing using ERP methodology across multiple languages (English, Mandarin, German). This work contributed to understanding the neural basis of syntactic universals and cross-linguistic variation.

Major Outcomes:

  • 3 peer-reviewed publications in high-impact journals
  • Presented at 8 international conferences
  • Established new ERP paradigms for cross-linguistic syntax research
  • Created open dataset for future cross-linguistic studies
